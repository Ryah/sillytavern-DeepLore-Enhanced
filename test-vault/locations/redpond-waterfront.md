# Redpond Waterfront

The developed shoreline of the [[Redpond]] lake, where the [[Redpond Fishers Cooperative]] maintains its docks, sorting facilities, and the cooperative hall. The waterfront also serves as an informal gathering point for Redpond residents in good weather. The lake is small enough that you can see across it from any point on the shore.

The docks are simple wooden structures — five piers extending into the shallow water, each wide enough for two small boats to moor side by side. The sorting facility is a long, open-sided shed where the day's catch is cleaned, weighed, and distributed, and the smell of it carries across the entire waterfront: fish scales, lake water, and the sharp brine of the preservation barrels lined up along the back wall. Cooperative members work the sorting tables from dawn until the catch is processed, their hands fast and practiced, their conversations carrying across the water in the flat acoustics of the lakeshore. Gulls circle overhead in numbers that suggest an optimism not supported by the cooperative's modest hauls.

The cooperative hall sits at the waterfront's center, a single-story stone building with a wide front porch where members gather before and after shifts. Inside: a common room with a hearth, a records office where the cooperative's ledgers are maintained with the meticulous care of an organization that knows its survival depends on transparent accounting, and a small kitchen that produces the fish stew served at every cooperative meeting. The hall doubles as a community center for the broader Redpond district — residents without cooperative membership use it for neighborhood meetings, holiday gatherings, and the informal dispute resolution that keeps most of Redpond's disagreements out of the [[Solarguard]]'s records.

In good weather, the waterfront comes alive in the evenings. Families spread blankets on the grassy shore, children wade in the shallows, and the cooperative members who finished the day's work sit on the pier ends with their feet hanging over the water, watching the light change on the lake's surface. It is one of the few places in Duskfrost where the city's constant political tension is genuinely absent — not suppressed, not ignored, but simply irrelevant to people whose primary concern is whether tomorrow's catch will be better than today's.
