# Amberburgh Trade Customs

Amberburgh trade runs on three parallel systems: the official Triumvirate-regulated market, the guild-mediated secondary market, and the night market. The customs governing each are distinct and occasionally contradictory. The one universal rule: a witnessed handshake in Amberburgh is legally binding regardless of whether any paperwork follows. This custom predates the [[Duskfrost Triumvirate]] and has survived every attempt to replace it with something more formal.

The official market operates from the Amberburgh Exchange, a converted pre-Calamity warehouse whose original purpose no one has successfully identified. Transactions above fifty crowns require a licensed witness from the Commerce Board. Transactions involving arcane materials require an additional Academy certification stamp, which costs twelve crowns and takes three days to obtain — a delay that has single-handedly sustained the secondary market. The guild-mediated market handles everything the official market makes inconvenient, which is most things. Guild brokers take a flat eight percent and guarantee delivery through their own enforcement networks, which are faster and considerably less polite than the Triumvirate courts.

Foreign merchants entering Amberburgh for the first time must register with the Harbor Authority and obtain a temporary trading license valid for sixty days. The license requires a local guarantor — a resident merchant who assumes liability for the foreigner's debts. Finding a guarantor costs between five and fifty crowns depending on the foreigner's reputation, species, and how desperate they look at the docks. [[Thyren]] merchants are exempt from the guarantor requirement under a treaty dating to Year 188; this exemption is resented by approximately everyone who is not a [[Thyren]].

The most contentious custom is the Rule of First Refusal: any goods entering Amberburgh by sea must be offered to local buyers before being transported inland. The rule was designed to ensure Amberburgh's market stayed competitive. In practice, it means local merchants can lowball incoming cargo with impunity, knowing the seller cannot legally walk away to find a better price in [[Bellsummit]] until the local offer has been formally declined, a process that takes — by remarkable coincidence — exactly long enough for the perishable goods to start deteriorating.
