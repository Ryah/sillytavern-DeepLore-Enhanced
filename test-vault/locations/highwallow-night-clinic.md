# Highwallow Night Clinic

A rotating unlicensed medical operation that runs out of three different [[Highwallow]] locations on alternating nights to avoid [[Wardenship]] attention. Staffed by two unlicensed practitioners, a retired Academy researcher, and whoever from the [[Highwallow Mutual Aid Society]] can be spared. Charges what patients can afford, which is frequently nothing.

The clinic's three locations are a basement beneath a shuttered bakery, a back room of the [[Highwallow Mutual Aid Hall]], and a converted storage space behind a chandler's shop — each equipped with the same portable kit of supplies that moves with the staff in canvas bags. The setup takes twenty minutes: a clean table, boiled instruments laid out on cloth, a lantern positioned for the best light the space allows, and a curtain rigged across the entrance for privacy. The smell varies by location but always includes the sharp bite of the antiseptic tincture the retired researcher mixes herself, the iron tang of blood, and whatever the room smelled like before the clinic arrived — flour, candle wax, damp stone.

Patients arrive after dark, guided by word of mouth and the quiet network of neighbors who know tonight's location. They come with infected wounds, persistent coughs, broken bones set badly by well-meaning family members, and the accumulated ailments of poverty — malnutrition, untreated infections, the damage of labor performed without adequate rest. The two unlicensed practitioners handle most of the routine work with steady hands and the confident competence of people who learned their trade by doing it. The retired Academy researcher handles the cases that require more knowledge — setting complicated fractures, treating infections that have spread beyond simple poultice work, and the occasional discreet consultation for conditions that carry legal consequences.

The [[Wardenship]] knows the clinic exists. They have not shut it down, partly because doing so would require acknowledging that Highwallow's residents lack adequate medical access, and partly because the retired researcher maintains a carefully calibrated relationship with a Wardenship inspector who understands that some problems are better solved by looking the other direction. The clinic's greatest vulnerability is not the law but supply — the antiseptic tincture requires ingredients the practitioners cannot always afford, and the bandages are whatever clean cloth the Mutual Aid Society can collect. On bad nights, they run out of both before they run out of patients.
