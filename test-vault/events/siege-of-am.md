# Siege of Am

In Year 78, the proto-Triumvirate dispatched approximately 300 soldiers — the largest military force Duskfrost had assembled since the Calamity — to absorb the independent settlement of Am, a farming community twenty miles inland that controlled a significant portion of the grain supply Duskfrost depended on. The logic was straightforward: Am had food, Duskfrost had soldiers, and the Triumvirate's leadership believed that a show of force would convince Am's council to accept incorporation without serious resistance. They were wrong.

Am had been founded by Calamity survivors who had specifically chosen to settle away from the emerging coastal power centers, and their resistance was informed by a deep institutional distrust of exactly the kind of consolidation Duskfrost represented. The settlement's defenses were modest — a timber palisade, a militia of perhaps eighty, and the advantage of interior lines — but Am's council had also stockpiled their grain reserves in dispersed underground caches that the besieging force could not locate or seize. The siege lasted forty-three days. Duskfrost's soldiers, operating at the end of a supply line they had not adequately planned, began running low on provisions before Am's defenders showed any sign of distress. The irony of starving while besieging a farming community was not lost on the troops.

The siege was broken not by a sortie but by a negotiation. Am's council offered terms: a guaranteed grain supply at fixed prices, exclusive trade partnership, and Duskfrost's formal recognition of Am's independence. The Triumvirate accepted because they had no practical alternative. Within three decades, the trade relationship had made Am economically dependent on Duskfrost's harbor access, and by Year 130, Am's council voluntarily petitioned for incorporation — on terms considerably less favorable than what they had originally been offered at spearpoint. Military historians cite the Siege of Am as the formative moment when the Triumvirate learned its most enduring lesson: economic absorption is slower than military conquest but considerably cheaper and far more permanent.

Veterans of the siege eventually settled in [[Bellsummit]], where their descendants still maintain a minor veterans' guild called the Am Returned. The guild's annual dinner features a traditional toast: "To the forty-three days we learned patience." The [[Champions of Patience]], founded fourteen years earlier, have never acknowledged any connection to this sentiment, but the timing is suggestive.
