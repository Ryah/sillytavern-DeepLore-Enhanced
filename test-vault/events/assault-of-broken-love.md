# Assault of Broken Love

The Assault of Broken Love was a skirmish during the late Calamity period, fought between two survivor groups contesting a freshwater spring in what would later become the eastern boundary of [[Highwallow]]. The name comes from the folk song cycle that preserved it — specifically, from the song's central image of a woman who killed her husband's brother over water rights and then carried the body to the spring so he could drink in death what he could not share in life. Whether this actually happened is debatable. That it became the defining story of the period is not.

The fighting lasted less than a day but killed an estimated thirty people — a significant number for a time when every able body represented irreplaceable survival capacity. The two groups had been cooperating for months before the dispute. The spring in question produced enough water for both. The conflict was not about scarcity but about the fear of future scarcity, a distinction the folk songs capture with uncomfortable precision. One verse, still sung in [[Highwallow]] taverns, translates roughly as: "We had enough today, so we killed for tomorrow, and tomorrow we had less."

The [[Battle of Vryhs]] occurred approximately two weeks later and involved some of the same survivors, now further reduced in number and fighting over a partially intact resonance tower they believed could be used for shelter. Historians of the Calamity period treat the two events as companion pieces — examples of how quickly cooperative survival groups fragmented once resources became contestable rather than shared. The folk songs survived through [[Zuvine]] oral tradition and were transcribed in Year 156 by an Academy ethnographer whose notes suggest she found the songs more historically reliable than any written Calamity-era source she had encountered.
