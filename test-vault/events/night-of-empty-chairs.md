# Night of Empty Chairs

On the evening of the 14th of Ashmonth, Year 178, three mid-tier Triumvirate functionaries — one from each house — were found dead in their respective offices within a span of two hours. Each had been killed by a different method: poison (the Velkhast tax assessor), blade (the Harcyne trade liaison), and arcane discharge (the Morveth cultural attaché). Each body was found seated at their desk with their chair pushed neatly to the table, as if they had simply stopped living mid-task. The coordinated timing and the deliberate variety of methods made the message unmistakable: someone had the capability to reach all three houses simultaneously, and they wanted the Triumvirate to know it.

The investigation consumed six months and produced no arrests. The [[Solarguard]], barely established at the time, lacked the resources for a case of this complexity. The [[Wardenship]] examined the arcane discharge that killed the Morveth functionary and determined it was not a standard sigil effect — the energy signature matched no known school of practice, a finding that was classified and has never been publicly released. The three victims had one thing in common besides their deaths: each had been involved in negotiating the terms of the [[Great Census of Year 180]], specifically the provisions governing which districts would be counted and by whom. Whether the census connection was motive or coincidence has never been established.

The Night of Empty Chairs — named for the three empty seats at the next Triumvirate council meeting — had two lasting consequences. First, it accelerated the development of the [[Wardenship]]'s protective detail protocols. Within a year, every senior Triumvirate official was assigned a Warden escort, a practice that has continued and expanded ever since. Second, it introduced a specific kind of fear into Duskfrost politics: the awareness that someone in the city had the resources and will to assassinate across all three houses simultaneously. This fear has influenced political behavior for over a century, though the identity of the assassin — or assassins — remains unknown.

The event's echoes are felt in the current [[Velkhast Succession Crisis]], where the first claimant's disappearance eight months ago has drawn uncomfortable parallels. Whether the comparison is apt or merely convenient for the remaining claimants' political narratives is a question that Duskfrost's historians are, understandably, reluctant to answer while the crisis is still unfolding.
