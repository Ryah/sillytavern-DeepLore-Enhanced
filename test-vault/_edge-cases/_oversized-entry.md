# Oversized Entry

The Vast Archive of Duskfrost stands as the most ambitious scholarly undertaking in the city's long and storied history. Founded three centuries ago by the archmage Thornwick Pallasys during the height of the Gilded Restoration, the Archive was conceived as a repository for every scrap of arcane knowledge recovered from the ruins of the pre-Calamity civilizations that once dominated the continent. Thornwick believed, perhaps naively, that if enough knowledge could be gathered in one place, the catastrophes of the past might be understood and ultimately prevented from repeating.

The building itself sprawls across three city blocks in the Bellsummit district, its exterior a chaotic assemblage of architectural styles representing the seven major construction phases it has undergone. The original tower, squat and practical, forms the core. Around it, successive generations of scholars added wings, annexes, reading rooms, cataloguing halls, and storage vaults, each in the style fashionable at the time of construction. The result is an architectural argument frozen in stone, a building that seems perpetually at war with itself.

Inside, the Archive is organized — loosely, its critics would say — into seventeen major sections corresponding to the broad categories of arcane inquiry recognized by the Duskfrost Academy. The first section, nearest the entrance, houses the public collection: texts on elementary conjuration, basic alchemical formulae, histories of the major noble houses, and maps of the known territories. These shelves are open to any literate citizen who applies for a reader's permit, pays the modest fee of two silver marks per season, and agrees not to damage or remove any materials.

Beyond the public section, accessed through a pair of heavy iron-banded doors watched at all hours by two Academy-trained wardens, lies the restricted collection. This is where the Archive earns its fearsome reputation. The restricted collection contains over forty thousand volumes, scrolls, and codices on topics ranging from the mildly dangerous to the utterly catastrophic. Soul-binding contracts. Phylactery construction manuals. Detailed taxonomies of demon princes with their true names annotated in the margins. Maps of the Hollow Roads — the network of tunnels said to connect the mortal realm to the planes below. And, most controversially, a complete set of the Pallasys Translations: Thornwick's own renderings of the Seventeen Seals, the pre-Calamity binding formulae that once imprisoned entities too powerful to destroy.

Access to the restricted collection requires a letter of endorsement from a senior Academy faculty member, a security deposit of fifty gold crowns, and agreement to a magical oath of confidentiality enforced by a geas bound to the reader's own sigil. Even with all of this in order, certain categories of material require additional approvals. The so-called Black Shelf — a single locked case containing eleven volumes — has not been accessed by any reader in sixty-three years, and the current Head Archivist, an elderly dwarf named Brimstone Kettleback, claims not to know what is in it. Most scholars believe this is a lie.

The staff of the Archive numbers forty-seven full-time employees and an ever-rotating cohort of Academy students serving their mandatory archival rotations. The full-time staff includes twelve senior archivists, each responsible for one or more of the major sections; twenty cataloguing clerks who spend their days transferring information from aging originals to preservation copies; eight wardens who provide security; four conservators who repair damaged materials; and three scribes of exceptional skill who handle requests for hand-copied excerpts when the original cannot be removed from the building. Presiding over all of them is Kettleback, who has held the position for thirty-one years and shows no sign of intending to retire.

The Archive's greatest institutional crisis in recent memory came eight years ago, when three volumes from the restricted collection disappeared without any record of them being checked out. The investigation that followed lasted two years, implicated seventeen individuals, and ultimately concluded without charges being filed against anyone. The missing volumes — a treatise on necromantic resonance theory, a collection of correspondence between two Calamity-era archmages, and a slim, untitled volume whose cover bore only a single eye drawn in silver ink — have never been recovered. Kettleback authorized the installation of additional wards following the incident, but rumors persist among the staff that the disappearances were not the result of theft at all, but rather that the books simply ceased to exist.

Students of the Academy are required to complete a minimum of forty hours of archival work before graduating, and most find the experience either formative or traumatic, depending on their temperament. The restricted section in particular has a reputation for inducing strange dreams in those who spend extended time among its shelves, a phenomenon that the Archive's own research staff has studied inconclusively for decades. The leading theory holds that certain texts emit a low-level psychic resonance that accumulates in confined spaces over time, but this explanation has never been proven to the satisfaction of the Academy's standards committee.

The Archive accepts donations of materials on a case-by-case basis, subject to review by a senior archivist. Most donated collections are assessed for duplication against existing holdings before acceptance, a process that can take months for large collections. Donors who attempt to contribute materials of questionable legality are politely but firmly refused, and in egregious cases, reported to the city watch. The Archive maintains strict neutrality in political matters and has historically survived regime changes, occupations, and at least one serious fire by making itself equally useful to every party in power.

Visiting scholars from outside Duskfrost are welcomed but must register with the city watch upon arrival and submit to a brief identity verification performed by one of the Archive's senior wardens. Foreign readers are limited to the public collection unless a special exemption is granted by the Head Archivist personally, which happens roughly twice per year.
