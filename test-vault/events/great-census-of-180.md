# Great Census of Year 180

The first systematic census of Duskfrost, ordered by the Triumvirate in Year 180, was an exercise in counting people who largely preferred not to be counted. The project took fourteen months, employed sixty-three census takers, and produced a final figure of approximately 52,000 residents across all districts — a number that everyone involved acknowledged was approximate at best and politically convenient at worst.

The census was motivated by a tax dispute between the three Triumvirate houses. Each house taxed its own territory and shared revenue according to population proportions established in the [[First Triumvirate Compact]]'s amendments, proportions that had been based on estimates rather than actual counts. House Velkhast, controlling the harbor and commercial districts with the densest population, argued they were subsidizing the other two houses and demanded a recount. The other houses agreed, expecting the numbers to confirm their own claims. No one's expectations were met cleanly.

The [[Highwallow]] count was disputed immediately. Census takers assigned to the district reported that several blocks in the Scar-adjacent zone were either inaccessible (structural collapse, arcane hazards) or actively hostile to enumeration. Two census takers were assaulted. One disappeared for three days and returned with no memory of where she had been, a phenomenon the [[Wardenship]] investigated and attributed to "residual Scar interference" without further explanation. The official Highwallow population of 8,400 is generally considered an undercount by at least 2,000, possibly more — the Scar-zone residents who refused to be counted had their own reasons, and those reasons have never been officially acknowledged.

The census also produced the first reliable count of non-human species in Duskfrost: approximately 3,200 [[Zuvine]] (surface-dwelling only; the underwater population was not counted), 1,800 [[Gnaino]], 900 [[Thyren]], and smaller populations of [[Kethvali]], [[Lorant]], and others. These numbers became the baseline for species-representation arguments that continue to this day.

Census records have become politically relevant again during the [[Velkhast Succession Crisis]], as competing claimants cite population figures — now 130 years out of date — to legitimize claims of district-level support. Proposals for a new census have been blocked by all three houses, each for different reasons, none of which they are willing to state publicly.
