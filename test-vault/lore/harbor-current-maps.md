# Harbor Current Maps

The [[Bayside Shushail]] current maps are maintained by the Harbor Authority and updated after every significant storm event. The harbor has three primary approach channels; the northern channel is impassable from late autumn through early spring due to current inversion caused by the underwater thermal vents first mapped in Year 244. All vessels entering at night are required to carry a licensed harbor pilot.

The central channel is the primary commercial approach — wide enough for two large vessels to pass, with a well-marked bottom clear of debris to a depth of twelve fathoms. The southern channel is shallower and narrower, used primarily by fishing boats and small coastal traders who know the route well enough to navigate the submerged pre-Calamity foundations that jut up from the harbor floor at irregular intervals. The Harbor Authority has marked the worst hazards with painted buoys, but the foundations shift — or appear to shift — after major arcane weather events, requiring re-survey. The southern channel has claimed an average of two vessels per year for the last century, a rate the Harbor Authority considers acceptable and the vessel owners' families do not.

The current maps themselves are works of practical art — hand-drawn on treated hide by the Authority's cartographic staff, updated with annotations in red ink after each survey. A complete set runs to forty-seven charts covering the harbor, approaches, and the coastal waters within a day's sail. The charts include not only current patterns and depth soundings but annotations on arcane interference zones where compass readings become unreliable. These zones correspond, roughly, to the locations of the [[Deep Harbor Ecology|thermal vents]] and the submerged pre-Calamity structures. Licensed harbor pilots memorize the interference zones; unlicensed navigators discover them the hard way.

The night-pilot requirement dates to Year 267, after a merchant convoy entering without pilots ran aground on a debris field that the morning survey showed had not been there the previous day. The debris appeared to be freshly deposited pre-Calamity metalwork, raised from the harbor bottom by an unknown mechanism. The Harbor Authority's official report attributed it to "seismic activity." The harbor pilots, who navigate these waters nightly, attribute it to something in the deep harbor that occasionally rearranges its surroundings. They do not speculate about what. The pilot licensing fee is thirty crowns per year, and there is no shortage of applicants despite the occupational hazards, because a licensed pilot earns more per voyage than most [[Amberburgh]] merchants earn in a week.
