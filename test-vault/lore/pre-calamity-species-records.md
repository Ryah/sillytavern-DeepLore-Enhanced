# Pre-Calamity Species Records

The [[Thornwick Pallasys|Pallasys Translations]] suggest the Antecedent Culture was multispecies, with at least six distinct populations represented in the resonance tower administrative records. Which of these correspond to any currently existing species is contested — the translation accuracy for species-descriptive terms is among the lowest in the [[Antecedent Script]] corpus, estimated at 35%.

The six populations are identified in the administrative records by single-character designations that Pallasys translated, with admitted uncertainty, as "the tall ones," "the deep ones," "the woven ones," "the bright ones," "the old ones," and "the silent ones." These translations are educated guesses at best. "The deep ones" might refer to the [[Gnaino]] ancestors — the character includes a sub-element associated with underground or enclosed spaces — but the Gnaino themselves neither confirm nor deny this, finding the entire exercise of matching ancient labels to living peoples presumptuous. "The tall ones" has been tentatively mapped to human populations. "The woven ones" may refer to the [[Thyren]], based on a character element that could mean "plant" or "growth" or "patience," depending on context. The remaining three designations resist even speculative identification.

The administrative records indicate that the six populations occupied different roles within the resonance tower network — not hierarchically, but functionally. Different populations appear to have been responsible for different aspects of the network's maintenance and operation. One population (possibly "the bright ones") is referenced exclusively in connection with the tower calibration protocols. Another ("the silent ones") appears only in records related to the [[Seven Seals]]. This functional specialization implies either a highly organized cooperative civilization or a deeply stratified one; the records do not distinguish between the two, and the difference matters enormously.

The [[Duskfrost Academy]] natural history department is the primary institution studying these records, though their work is hampered by the fundamental problem that the Antecedent Culture may have classified species differently than modern taxonomy does. Several researchers have argued that the six designations do not refer to biological species at all but to social castes, professional guilds, or even stages of life — a theory that would explain why the species-descriptive terms translate so poorly. If the Antecedent Culture did not think in terms of species, trying to map their categories onto modern species is asking the wrong question. The department has been arguing about which question to ask instead for approximately forty years.
