# Deep Harbor Ecology

The [[Bayside Shushail]] harbor supports an unusual mix of species concentrated near the thermal vent field at its center. Several species found here exist nowhere else in the known coast — possibly descended from populations that survived the Calamity in the thermal plumes. Fishers report that the deep-water catch changes character approximately every seven years in ways that correlate, loosely, with the [[Resonance Scar Properties|Resonance Scar]]'s measurement cycles.

The thermal vents themselves predate the Calamity — geological surveys suggest they have been active for millennia, heated by a magma intrusion that may or may not be natural. The Academy's geology department suspects the vents were deliberately created or enhanced by the Antecedent Culture as part of their infrastructure, possibly as a heat sink for the resonance network. This would explain why the vent temperatures fluctuate in patterns that track arcane activity rather than geological cycles. The harbor bottom around the vents is littered with pre-Calamity debris — corroded metal frameworks, stone foundations, and at least one structure that sonar mapping suggests is intact but buried under forty meters of sediment. The Harbor Authority has prohibited salvage operations in the vent zone since Year 260, after a [[Zuvine]] dive team surfaced with burns that no thermal exposure should have caused.

The unique species are the harbor's most commercially significant feature. The deep-harbor luminous eel, found nowhere else, produces a bioluminescent oil that burns cleaner and longer than conventional lamp oil. The harvest is strictly regulated — twelve licensed boats, seasonal quotas, mandatory size limits — because the eels reproduce slowly and the harbor's ecosystem is poorly understood. The Academy's natural history department has catalogued forty-seven species unique to the vent field, including a species of crab whose shell incorporates trace amounts of the same unidentifiable metal found in pre-Calamity artifacts. Whether the crabs are eating the debris or have evolved to metabolize whatever the debris leaks is an open research question.

The seven-year catch cycle is the detail that troubles the Academy most. Every seven years, approximately, the deep-water species shift — some become abundant, others vanish entirely, and one or two species appear that have never been documented before. The cycle aligns loosely with fluctuation patterns measured at the [[Resonance Scar Properties|Resonance Scar]] in [[Highwallow]], hundreds of miles inland. The implication — that the harbor's ecosystem and the Scar are connected through some remnant of the pre-Calamity resonance network — is one the Academy has been carefully not publishing while they gather more data. The fishers, who do not need Academy publications to notice patterns, have been adjusting their practices to the cycle for generations.
