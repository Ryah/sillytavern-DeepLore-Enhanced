# Arcane Bloodline Inheritance

Arcane affinity does not follow simple inheritance rules. Strong affinity in both parents does not guarantee affinity in children; two non-affinity parents occasionally produce a child with exceptional talent. The Academy's current model treats arcane affinity as polygenic with significant environmental modulation — a description that explains the data without explaining anything useful about prediction.

The [[Duskfrost Academy]] maintains the most comprehensive genealogical records tracking arcane affinity across generations — over four centuries of birth records, affinity test results, and family lineage charts filling an entire wing of the Archive. The data reveals patterns that tantalize without resolving: affinity clusters in certain families for three or four generations, then vanishes. It appears in isolated individuals with no family history whatsoever, disproportionately in communities near ley line convergence points. Children born in the [[Resonance Scar]]-adjacent tenements of [[Highwallow]] show elevated affinity rates, which could indicate environmental activation or could indicate that people with latent affinity are unconsciously drawn to high-resonance areas. The Academy has been arguing about which interpretation is correct since Year 203.

Cross-species inheritance adds further complications. [[Selaveth]]-human pairings produce children with unpredictable affinity profiles — sometimes exceeding either parent, sometimes showing none at all. [[Thyren]] bloodlines carry affinity markers that the Academy's standard testing cannot reliably detect, leading to a decades-long dispute about whether Thyren arcane abilities constitute "affinity" in the same sense the term applies to humans. The [[Zuvine]] do not participate in Academy genealogical studies, considering the entire framework reductive. They are probably right, but this does not help the Academy's statisticians.

The political implications are significant. Several [[Bellsummit]] families have used genealogical records to argue for hereditary claims to Academy positions, asserting that their bloodlines produce reliable arcane talent. The data does not support this claim with any statistical rigor. The families make it anyway. The Academy's official position — that affinity cannot be predicted or bred for — is technically honest and strategically useful, as it prevents any single family from claiming a monopoly on magical talent. Whether the Academy would maintain this position if the data changed is a question no one asks in polite company.
