# Attack of Lost Friends

A Calamity-era conflict preserved in folk memory as the "attack of lost friends" — the disintegration of established alliances as the grid collapse destroyed the social order that maintained them. Not a single battle but a pattern: people who had been allies for years turning on each other as survival pressures mounted.

The [[Zuvine Song Cycles]] contain the most detailed accounts, encoding specific betrayals in melodic sequences that elder singers can recite but refuse to translate for outsiders. What fragments have been shared describe a sequence that repeated across communities: first the food stores failed, then the water purification sigils stopped working, then the defensive wards collapsed. Within days of losing basic infrastructure, communities that had cooperated for decades fractured along lines no one had previously considered important — species, district of origin, which trade they practiced. The songs name specific individuals. Some of those names belong to families still prominent in Duskfrost. The elders' refusal to translate is not cultural reticence; it is diplomatic caution.

The psychological dimension is what gives the event its lasting cultural weight. The Calamity killed millions through infrastructure collapse, arcane cascade, and simple starvation. But the Attack of Lost Friends is remembered differently — not as catastrophe but as revelation. The bonds people trusted most proved to be the most fragile. Neighbors who had shared meals for years fought over grain stores. Colleagues who had collaborated on the resonance towers sabotaged each other's evacuation routes. The Reconstruction-era founders cited the Attack explicitly in drafting the [[Binding Compact]], arguing that formal legal structures were necessary precisely because informal trust had proven worthless under pressure.

Some historians consider this a precursor event to the [[War of Glimmering Hope]], noting that the current political tensions follow an eerily similar trajectory: long-standing cooperation degrading as a scarce resource — in this case, the resonance grid fragment beneath [[Rappiop Grove]] — makes cooperation more costly than competition. The parallel is imperfect. The differences are not reassuring.
