# Antecedent Script

The Antecedent Culture used a logographic script with approximately 2,400 base characters and an estimated 40,000 compound characters. [[Thornwick Pallasys]] spent thirty years producing partial translations. Modern scholars estimate he achieved approximately 60% accuracy. The remaining 40% has resisted four centuries of effort.

The script operates on a layered principle that modern linguists find maddening: each base character carries a primary meaning, but its meaning shifts depending on the characters adjacent to it, the medium it is inscribed on, and — according to some researchers — the ambient arcane conditions at the time of writing. This last claim is contentious, but it would explain why identical inscriptions found at different sites appear to say different things. The Academy's linguistics department has burned through eleven department heads in the last century, each retiring with some variation of "the script is not a language, it is a conversation, and we are only hearing one side."

The most reliable translations come from administrative and technical documents — inventory lists, construction specifications, and what appear to be maintenance logs for the resonance tower network. These use a restricted subset of approximately 400 characters in standardized combinations. Poetry and philosophical texts, which use the full character set with apparent creative license, remain almost entirely opaque. The [[Scribe Fragment — Lost Translation]] is one of the few narrative texts translated with any confidence, and even that relies heavily on context rather than literal character-by-character decoding.

[[Gorduin Wynlar]] maintains a private collection of Antecedent fragments that he considers too fragile for general access. This is technically true — the oldest fragments crumble at incautious handling. It is also convenient, as several fragments in his collection appear to contain information about the [[Seven Seals]] that the [[Oracles of Might]] have not made available to the broader Academy. Gorduin's position is that the fragments are too fragile, not too dangerous. His colleagues believe him on neither count.
