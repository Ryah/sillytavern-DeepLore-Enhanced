# Redpond Fishers Cooperative

The Redpond Fishers Cooperative holds the city license for freshwater fishing in the [[Redpond]] lake district. Small operation — eighteen members, four boats, and a processing shed that smells exactly how you'd expect — but they supply a meaningful fraction of the city's freshwater fish. The [[Harcyne Granary Authority]] has been trying to absorb them for twenty years, which the fishers consider both flattering and a threat to their existence.

The Cooperative's value is disproportionate to its size. In a city where the [[Harcyne Granary Authority]] controls every grain shipment and every preserved-meat contract, freshwater fish from Redpond is the one significant food source that doesn't pass through Harcyne hands. This makes eighteen people hauling perch and pike out of a modest lake more politically important than any of them wanted to be. Kellan Marsh, the Cooperative's elected chair and a third-generation fisher who communicates primarily through grunts and meaningful silences, handles the political dimension by refusing to engage with it. When Harcyne representatives visit to discuss "partnership opportunities," Marsh listens politely, says he'll bring it to the membership, and the membership votes no. This has happened nine times.

The fishing itself is seasonal and weather-dependent. Peak harvest runs from late spring through early autumn; winter fishing is possible but yields drop by half. The Cooperative sells directly to market stalls in [[Amberburgh]] and [[Rimeborough]], bypassing the [[Amberburgh Merchants League]]'s licensing requirements through a municipal exemption for "primary producers" that the League has tried to revoke twice. The fishers also maintain an informal arrangement with the [[Highwallow Mutual Aid Society]]: unsold catch at the end of market day goes to Highwallow at cost, which means at a loss, which the Cooperative absorbs because most of them grew up poor enough to remember what hunger tastes like.

The Cooperative's tension is generational. The older fishers — Marsh's generation — view the operation as a way of life and a civic duty. The younger members, inheriting boats and licenses from parents who are retiring or dying, see the economics more clearly: the work is hard, the margins are thin, and the Harcyne buyout offers get more generous each year. Marsh knows that when enough older members retire, the vote will eventually go the other way. He fishes anyway. The lake doesn't care about economics, and neither does he.
