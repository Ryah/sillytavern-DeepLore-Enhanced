# Ye Olde Guinea Pig Inn

A well-regarded inn in [[East Hamp District|East Hamp]] with clean rooms, reasonable prices, and an unexplained guinea pig motif throughout its decor. Popular with [[Marblesmiths]] guild members and traveling tradespeople. Does not actually serve guinea pig.

The inn is a three-story timber-and-stone building on East Hamp's main street, identifiable by the carved wooden guinea pig above the entrance — a plump, cheerful creature rendered with a craftsmanship that suggests the carver took the commission more seriously than the subject warranted. Inside, the common room is warm and well-kept: swept floors, a large hearth, tables worn smooth by years of elbows, and guinea pig imagery in every conceivable medium — painted on the walls, carved into the stair banisters, embroidered on the seat cushions, etched into the mugs. The effect is either charming or unsettling depending on the visitor's tolerance for rodent-themed hospitality. The air smells of the kitchen's perpetual pot of stew, fresh bread, and the beeswax the staff uses on the wooden surfaces with devoted regularity.

The rooms upstairs are small, clean, and furnished with the practical simplicity that working travelers prefer: a bed, a washstand, a hook for a coat, a window that opens. The mattresses are stuffed with wool — not straw, a distinction the innkeeper mentions to every guest — and the linens are changed between visitors, a standard that East Hamp's other boarding houses do not consistently match. Rates are posted on a board behind the bar and do not fluctuate by season, a pricing philosophy the innkeeper defends with the argument that fairness is more profitable than opportunism, given enough time.

The Marblesmiths treat the Guinea Pig as an unofficial guild annex — apprentices celebrate their advancement here, journeymen negotiate contracts over dinner, and master smiths hold court at the corner table that is never formally reserved but is always available for them. Traveling tradespeople, particularly those visiting East Hamp for the guild's seasonal procurement fairs, fill the remaining rooms and add enough variety to the common room's conversation to keep it from becoming exclusively a Marblesmiths' club. The origin of the guinea pig motif remains unexplained. The innkeeper, when asked, smiles and changes the subject with a practiced deflection that suggests the mystery is worth more to the business than any answer could be.
