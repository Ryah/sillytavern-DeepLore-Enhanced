# Guild Hierarchy Laws

Duskfrost recognizes four guild tiers: Licensed, Registered, Sponsored, and Charter. Charter guilds have independent legal standing and can bring suit in [[Duskfrost Triumvirate]] courts directly. Sponsored guilds require a Charter guild patron. Disputes between guilds of different tiers are adjudicated by the Commerce Board, which is widely regarded as impartial by Charter guilds and as owned by Charter guilds by everyone else.

The tier system was established during the Reconstruction era as a compromise between the Triumvirate's desire for regulatory control and the merchant class's insistence on self-governance. Licensed guilds — the lowest tier — may operate a single trade in a single district, subject to annual inspection and renewal fees. Registered guilds may operate across districts but cannot enforce their own contracts; they must use Triumvirate courts. Sponsored guilds gain limited self-governance under the umbrella of a Charter patron, including the right to maintain their own dispute resolution panels for internal matters. The sponsorship relationship is supposed to be protective. In practice, Charter guilds extract between ten and twenty-five percent of their sponsored guilds' revenue in exchange for the privilege, a rate that the Commerce Board has reviewed and found "within acceptable parameters" every time it has been challenged.

Charter guilds — currently numbering seven in Duskfrost — are the apex of the system. They maintain their own courts, enforce their own contracts (including [[Blood Binding Contracts|blood-binding]]), and negotiate directly with the Triumvirate on trade policy. The seven Charter guilds collectively control approximately sixty percent of Duskfrost's legitimate commerce. The [[Amberburgh Merchants League]] is the largest; the Bellsummit Artificers' Guild is the wealthiest per member; the [[Bonehands]] technically hold a Charter but operate in a legal gray zone that the Commerce Board has decided is someone else's problem.

Advancement between tiers requires meeting financial thresholds, demonstrating operational capacity, and — for the jump from Sponsored to Charter — securing a majority vote from the existing Charter guilds. This last requirement is the system's most effective bottleneck: no new Charter guild has been approved in forty-seven years. The existing seven have a shared interest in keeping the club exclusive. Three applications are currently pending; the oldest has been "under review" for eleven years. The applicants have begun exploring whether the [[Binding Compact]] contains any mechanism to compel a vote, which is making the Charter guilds very nervous and the Triumvirate's legal scholars very busy.
