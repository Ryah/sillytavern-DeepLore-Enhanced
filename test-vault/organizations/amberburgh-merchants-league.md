# Amberburgh Merchants League

The Amberburgh Merchants League is the trade association covering most commercial activity in the market district. It mediates disputes, lobbies the [[Duskfrost Triumvirate]] on tariff policy, and runs the licensed market stalls. Membership is mandatory for established traders; the fee is controversial and rises every year by an amount the League describes as "modest" and its members describe with less diplomatic language.

The League operates from the second floor of the [[Amberburgh Exchange]], where Guildmaster Pellen Drossweir holds court in an office that smells of ink, lamp oil, and the particular anxiety of people waiting to learn whether their tariff petition has been approved. Drossweir is a third-generation merchant with the political instincts of a much more interesting person trapped in a very boring job. He maintains cordial relations with all three Triumvirate houses, favors none of them publicly, and accepts gifts from all of them privately — not because he is corrupt, but because refusing gifts from powerful people is a form of insult he cannot afford.

The League's core function is market regulation: stall assignments, quality inspections, dispute arbitration, and the enforcement of pricing standards that prevent the kind of undercutting that collapsed the fish market in Year 302. This last function brings them into chronic friction with the [[Night Market Guild]], whose unlicensed vendors undercut League prices on everything from textiles to alchemical basics. The League has petitioned the Triumvirate three times to shut down the night market. Three times the petition has stalled, because the Triumvirate privately benefits from a market that keeps the [[Orvathi]] community economically integrated without requiring anyone to officially acknowledge it.

The League's internal politics are straightforward: the wealthier members want lower tariffs on imports (which helps their supply chains), the smaller members want higher tariffs (which protects their local production), and Drossweir splits the difference with annual rate adjustments that satisfy no one equally, which he considers the definition of fair governance. The [[Velkhast Trading Company]]'s paralysis during the succession dispute has created an opportunity — harbor berth access, normally controlled by Velkhast, is temporarily available to League members willing to negotiate directly with dockworkers. Several merchants have done so. Drossweir pretends not to notice, because acknowledging it would require him to have an opinion about the Velkhast succession, and opinions about noble house disputes are how merchants stop being merchants.
