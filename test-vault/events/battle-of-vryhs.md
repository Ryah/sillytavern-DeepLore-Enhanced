# Battle of Vryhs

The Battle of Vryhs was fought over the partially intact ruins of a resonance tower approximately two weeks after the [[Assault of Broken Love]], involving many of the same survivors now further diminished in number and increasingly desperate. The tower — designated "Vryhs" after the pre-Calamity settlement that had surrounded it — still held partial structural integrity and, more importantly, still emitted a low-level resonance field that kept the surrounding area marginally warmer than the blasted landscape beyond. In the Calamity's aftermath, warmth was worth killing for.

Three survivor factions converged on the tower simultaneously. The largest group, approximately forty people led by a former Antecedent Culture military officer whose name survives only as "the Commander," attempted to claim the tower by establishing a defensive perimeter. A second group of mixed-species survivors, including several [[Zuvine]] who had been driven from their flooded coastal shelters, approached from the south. The third group is described in the folk songs only as "the ones who came from below" — possibly survivors who had sheltered in the [[Hollow Roads]] and emerged near the tower. The fighting was close-quarters, chaotic, and lasted most of a day. The Commander's group held the tower but lost over half their number doing so.

The tower itself proved less useful than hoped. Its resonance field was erratic, causing headaches, nosebleeds, and in at least two cases, sudden seizures in those who slept within it. The survivors abandoned it within a month and moved south toward what would become the first permanent Reconstruction settlements. The tower collapsed entirely sometime in the following decade — by the time Academy surveyors reached the site in Year 87, only the foundation stones remained, still faintly warm to the touch.

Historians cite Vryhs alongside the [[Assault of Broken Love]] as evidence that the Calamity's social destruction was as thorough as its physical destruction. The folk song cycle ends with a verse that scholars have debated for centuries: "The tower stood, and we broke ourselves against it, and when we left, it fell." Whether this is literal description or metaphor for the Antecedent Culture's fate is an argument that has fueled more than one academic career.
