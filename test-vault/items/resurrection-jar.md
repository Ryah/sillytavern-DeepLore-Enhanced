# Resurrection Jar

A ceramic jar of ancient make that allegedly holds the preserved essence of a soul after death, preventing final departure for a limited window. The window is believed to be 72 hours. No successful demonstration has been recorded. The jar is currently in private collection in [[Bellsummit]].

The jar is approximately twenty centimeters tall, made of a dark red ceramic with a surface texture that the [[Duskfrost Academy]]'s materials department describes as "organic" — not smooth like fired pottery but textured like dried tissue, with visible grain patterns and a slight give under pressure that ceramic should not have. The lid is sealed with a wax-like substance that has resisted four centuries of drying and shows no signs of degradation. [[Antecedent Script]] characters circle the jar's widest point; [[Gorduin Wynlar]]'s translation reads: "The house between the door and the road." This is either a description of the jar's function (a container for souls in transit between death and whatever comes after) or a poetic label that means something entirely different in a cultural context no living person understands.

The 72-hour window is inferred from the script's secondary inscriptions, which reference a time period Pallasys translated as "three suns" and which subsequent scholars have refined to "three full day-night cycles." The implication is that a soul captured by the jar can be preserved for 72 hours, after which it departs regardless — to wherever souls go, a topic on which the pre-Calamity texts are deliberately unhelpful. The jar's use case, if functional, would be resurrection: preserving the soul long enough to repair or replace the body, then reuniting the two. This is not known to be possible. It is also not known to be impossible, which is why the jar commands a private collection price estimated at over ten thousand crowns.

Like the [[Divine Tiara]], its provenance suggests Antecedent Culture manufacture, though the craftsmanship is notably different — cruder, more functional, less aesthetically refined. The Academy's comparative analysis suggests the jar was made for practical use rather than display, which implies the Antecedent Culture considered soul preservation a practical activity. The current collector — a Bellsummit aristocrat who acquired it through the [[Amberburgh]] auction house — has declined all requests to test the jar, including a notably indelicate proposal from the Academy's thanatology researcher to "arrange a controlled death scenario." The collector's position is that the jar is a historical curiosity. The collector's security arrangements — three ward layers, a [[Nullifying Gauntlet]]-equipped guard, and a sealed room with no windows — suggest the collector considers it considerably more than that.
